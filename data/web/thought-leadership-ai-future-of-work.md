# Cognizant — Thought Leadership: AI and the Future of Work

Sources: Cognizant Insights ("The Work Ahead" / research library, "New work, new world 2026", "The skills reset" blog), and reporting from Fortune (June 2026). Content rephrased for compliance; see links for originals.

## "New work, new world" research (flagship AI-and-jobs study)
- 2023 edition (with Oxford Economics): predicted that by 2032, ~90% of jobs would be affected by generative AI, with roughly 10% facing transformational change. The headline message: businesses that invest in people will unlock AI's full power.
- 2026 update ("New work, new world 2026: How AI is reshaping work faster than expected"): the disruption is arriving faster and broader than predicted. Cognizant's data shows ~93% of jobs are already "AI-capable," and ~30% now face fundamental/existential change. In effect, the 2032 threshold was reached around 2026, roughly six years early.
- AI "exposure scores" rose sharply across the board: e.g., construction roles moved from ~4% to ~12%, while CEO/C-suite exposure jumped from ~25% to ~60%, showing white-collar and leadership roles are far from immune.
- Related framing: AI can already perform tasks worth on the order of $4.5 trillion of US labor, but capturing that value requires intentional skilling, context, and focus (Cognizant argues there is no near-term "AI bubble" burst).
- Cognizant research leaders associated with this work include Ollie O'Donoghue (head of research) and Sushant Warikoo (chief business officer of AI).

## Core stance: tasks, not roles
- Cognizant's central argument is that AI changes the nature of tasks far more often than it eliminates entire roles. As tasks evolve, human judgment, adaptability, and contextual understanding become more valuable, not less.
- Mid-career and experienced professionals are pivotal: their institutional knowledge and domain expertise, amplified by AI, become a performance accelerator. Concentrating AI use among a few specialists creates capability bottlenecks.

## "The skills reset" (April 2026 perspective, by Thomas Mathew, VP, Head of ASEAN & Greater China)
- Productivity at scale comes from expanding, not narrowing, the pool of people who can work with AI.
- The skills reset is a growth agenda, not just a training initiative. Three outcomes when non-specialists are enabled with real workflows (plus coaching and guardrails): productivity accelerates, innovation increases, and growth becomes more resilient because capability is distributed.
- "Trust in AI grows through use" — familiarity builds confidence, which drives adoption. Usage metrics are a useful first baseline (low usage signals an awareness, confidence, or tool-fit problem).
- Tied to Synapse (skilling 2 million people by 2030) and "AI for Impact Community Labs" hands-on enablement workshops.
- What leaders should do now: expand AI fluency beyond technical teams; prioritize hands-on enablement; empower mid-career talent; design operating models for continuous learning and distributed innovation.

## CEO Ravi Kumar S — public positions (June 2026)
- Argues AI can broaden opportunity if deployed to augment workers rather than replace them; says Cognizant has increased hiring of graduates and early-career workers (reportedly hiring 20,000+ graduates in the year).
- Describes a "flat-earth pyramid": the traditional talent pyramid flattens, with leaner middle layers but many new jobs at the "front" and "back" of AI workflows — validation, verification, and authentication roles around AI.
- Calls AI "tokenmaxxing" (maximizing token usage) a "vanity metric" — emphasizing business value over raw AI consumption.

## Adjacent research themes (Cognizant Insights / "The Work Ahead" library)
- Closing the AI execution gap: two-thirds of leaders have not yet shown measurable productivity gains from AI; a quarter have paused/abandoned deployments. Firms with mature tech infrastructure plus focused AI investment are pulling ahead (billions in annual returns).
- Why "AI Builders" are essential: you can't just pull an off-the-shelf model to solve complex enterprise problems; value must be built, not bought.
- The agentic internet and "agent experience": businesses must build design, technical, and organizational capabilities for a web where AI agents are first-class participants.
- AI's two-year timeline / legacy modernization mandate: tech debt in legacy systems is now the bottleneck to AI value; a "flywheel" modernization strategy is needed.
- Think like an AI-native business; rethink the talent pyramid in the age of gen AI.
- Responsible/confident AI adoption: strong leadership commitment but gaps in business readiness; only about a third of consumers trust gen AI, so trust-building is essential.

## Why this matters for an ACE / AI-builder interview
Expect Cognizant interviewers to value candidates who can speak to: AI augmenting (not just replacing) people; the "tasks not roles" framing; building enterprise-grade AI rather than buying off-the-shelf; responsible/governed AI; agentic AI and the agent experience; and the legacy-modernization-as-prerequisite-for-AI argument. These mirror Cognizant's own published positions.
